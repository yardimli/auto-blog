1. Purpose, Educational Methods, and Teaching Location

Purpose

We hope that during his learning process, he will have the opportunity to use languages he is familiar with in daily life, whether it's Mandarin, Turkish, sign language, or other communication methods. We want to continue providing him with an environment where he can use skills he is familiar with to learn and share ideas.

Extending from this point, we believe that knowledge and skills that are important and practical for his life should be integrated into the teaching methods, incorporating life experiences. This allows him to have choices in learning methods and understand how to express himself.

We expect him to gain new experiences from learning activities, whether psychological or physical, helping him learn to adapt and cope with his specific needs in life. We hope he can freely develop his personality.

Educational Methods

We need to give him opportunities to learn from mistakes. If the content is suitable, we can take turns answering or switch roles to make him feel the atmosphere of collaborative learning. If Yi-Luo shows boredom, impatience, or inability to understand the teaching method, we must change our teaching approach or the subject matter.

He will acquire knowledge from physical objects, computer-based teaching tools, or activities. If the teaching tools we need are unavailable on the market, we will make them ourselves. To achieve the best motivation for learning, every subject should have at least one alternative guidance method or thematic scenario prepared to adapt to Yi-Luo's mood, interests, and observed psychological growth at the time.

From homeschooling until now, his mind has been constantly growing, from being unable to participate in any learning activities to accepting this responsibility. There was a period when his emotions fluctuated greatly; during such times, we would find times when he was more psychologically balanced to have lessons, or try sitting quietly for 5 minutes before class, or walk/run on the treadmill for a few meters to calm down before starting. There was also a period when he couldn't complete an activity lasting at least 20 minutes; then, we had to observe which type of content was more suitable for this period, or divide the day into shorter segments for lessons. Sometimes Yi-Luo cared a lot about the lesson content; we would then observe his reaction to find the reason for his dislike, and sometimes it was simply because he had many changes in life he needed to overcome, so the lesson content needed to be easier and more repetitive.

Teaching Location

Yi-Luo has two places for learning activities: one at home, and one is a room we prepared for him at our company. Previously, we taught him new content both at home and in the company room. This year, we hope to use the company environment to cultivate his ability to independently complete assigned tasks, just like how he comes up with ideas for play.

Regarding music and visual arts courses, we still conduct them at home or in the outdoor garden. For physical activity, we have prepared a gymnastics room at home where he can do exercises requiring eye-hand-foot coordination and agility training, as well as activities that help him supplement sensory input or relieve excess sensory input at other times. We also use the sports fields and equipment provided in parks to let him experience different sports activities.

Extracurricular Teaching Location

To increase life experience, and learn and find coping mechanisms when encountering difficulties.

Personality Description

Yi-Luo is happy, observant, independent, and persistent. Compared to before, he has become more aware of his own feelings and environmental settings. We have also noticed him expressing, bit by bit in his own way, his desire for autonomy, such as sleeping until dawn by himself, and indicating that we should continue working in our workroom instead of sitting on the sofa in his room waiting with him. We will observe for a period, suggest he try different approaches, see his reaction, and then tell him our observations, guessing what emotions and cognitions he might be experiencing and how to describe them.

Often, Yi-Luo feels the need to discipline himself because the emotional ups and downs caused by major and minor life events are not simple. However, when faced with things we cannot change, such as the death due to kidney failure of a cat he had been with since childhood, Yi-Luo did not become as emotional and disruptive to his daily routine as we expected. Teaching Methods

Morning learning at home focuses on new content, while afternoon learning in the classroom prepared for him at our company emphasizes practicing independent completion of certain tasks. We adjust learning goals and schedules very flexibly according to Yi-Luo's individual condition at the time.

This spring, we started letting him try out a reward point system based on daily life activities during lessons. We observed that he could understand the purpose, so this year we will continue to expand the reward point system rules within curriculum activities. Reward points earned from daily life can be exchanged for toys he particularly wants, visits to special places he desires, reclaiming time for using his phone, iPad, or computer. The rules are limited to life tasks, such as remembering to regulate his emotions, remembering to communicate verbally first rather than using negative emotions when expressing needs or ideas, remembering not to express anger negatively if waking up suddenly at night, remembering to pick up toys from the floor and put them away before sleep, etc. The reward points from learning activities provide him with a quantifiable concept of his learning goals for the day, the week, or the month.

The reward point system is somewhat similar to earning course credits, but the rules are more detailed, so in some ways, it's like a supermarket point collection activity for rewards. Generally speaking, reward point tasks in the curriculum involve doing things related to the lessons, e.g., creating 10 sentences using specific color words earns 2 points, recognizing and practicing fraction multiplication calculations for 30 minutes earns 5 points. The number of reward points for each rule item is assessed based on difficulty and the time he takes to complete it, and they are also divided into two main categories: repeatable and non-repeatable tasks. This is because currently, the method we've tried with Yi-Luo is telling him that regardless of which rule item he chooses, the total points needed are 5; once he gets 5 points, the class is over. We've observed that he can understand this rule, which eases the pressure of learning. For example, when we remind him he still needs 3 points, his acceptance is much better than telling him he has 10 minutes left until class ends.

We first try out tasks with Yi-Luo to know how many reward points an item is worth. We hope he can spend at least 30 minutes participating in the lesson (totaling 5 points). If a task is difficult, completing it earns more points. If it's simple, he gets fewer points, requiring him to choose several more items. This gives him some psychological preparation and flexibility in planning. The reason for categorizing tasks as repeatable or non-repeatable is that we feel some items lose meaning if repeated continuously within 30 minutes, or he might lose concentration if repeating the same task over and over.

Currently, we first select which task to do because when we tried it with Yi-Luo, he was unwilling or unable to decide which item to choose. We hope he can gradually develop his own preferences regarding task selection.

Our teaching method is 1-on-1, but we hope to try involving extended family members to let him experience others' perspectives. We try to establish interactive ways that make the lessons interesting for him, allowing us to participate together in Yi-Luo's learning process.

Currently, learning content is primarily text and multimedia-based, with most lessons conducted on the computer. Regardless of the subject, if suitable, we watch together and take turns doing tasks. We design content that allows us and Yi-Luo to execute and achieve goals together, thereby increasing his learning motivation.

Besides Chinese and Turkish for language learning, Math, Science, Music, and Multimedia Design are taught in English, while extracurricular activities often occur in mixed-language environments.

Science, Music, and Visual Arts are integrated into life activities. Depending on the activity content, lessons might be in the classroom, kitchen, garden, home hardware workshop, or outdoor forest. Regardless of the subject, this academic year, we hope to strengthen the application of learned knowledge to daily life.

Learning Assessment Methods

Every lesson will have an assessment, or sometimes a record of reward points. These results can be converted to show the frequency of practice for each item within a subject, the time spent, the number of questions answered correctly, etc. Additionally, we use "Poor," "Okay," "Good" as assessment levels. Assessment items for each subject include "Attention," "Interest Expression," and "Learning Outcome," assessed daily and weekly. We also conduct a weekly summary and adjust the following week's lesson content based on this summary. All assessment results are recorded on paper and digitally.

5. Expected Outcomes

In terms of language, through practice in the curriculum, he will become increasingly familiar with organizing sentences, gain confidence, and make it a natural habit. Even in emotionally urgent situations, he will be able to use this communication ability. Furthermore, he will be able to use the language knowledge learned to explore and develop new interests and skills.

When he sees numbers, he will know they can be used for counting and calculation. He will be able to solve 3-digit addition or subtraction problems and understand the principles of multiplication and division. He will develop a digital sense of measurement and know that numbers play important roles in telling time and days of the week. He can pose associative mathematical problems in daily life.

In music and multimedia design, he can play simple songs and demonstrate an understanding of creation, regardless of the tools used. He can understand that art is a form of expression and communication, and creation is a medium for interacting with the world.

In science, he will learn to view the world he exists in from a physical perspective and know that various things can be measured. He will gain a better understanding of himself and his surroundings. By exploring science subjects, his horizons will expand beyond daily needs, and he will recognize the concept of cause and effect.
